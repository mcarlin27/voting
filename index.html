Voting!

So you want to vote, huh?

Good for you! The right to vote has been exercised in this country going back to 1776, though, of course, back then such rights were restricted to white, property-owning Protestant men. Non-Protestants were granted rights to vote by 1828, when Maryland became the last state to enfranchise (white) Jews. By 1856 the property-requirement had also been dropped, removing the last barier for white men to vote. By 1870 that right was extended to non-white males, though in practice many states passed so-called "grandfather clauses," which denied the vote to any man whose grandfather had been denied the vote himself (though these laws were themselves declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court in 1915). White women gained the vote in 1920, followed by Native Americans in 1948, and all other persons of legal voting age in 1965 with the passage of the Voting Rights Act. You can read more about the history of voting in America here.

How to vote:

  1. First, let's determine if you're eligible to vote:
    • Are you a citizen of the United States?
    • Do you meet your state's residency requirements? (Not sure, follow this link to find out.)
    • Are you currently 18 or older, or will you turn 18 on or before Election Day? Click here to find the date of your next local or Federal election, or follow this link to find your state's specific registration requirements.
    • Are you registered?

      • If you aren't eligible to vote in the U.S., click below for information about how else to get involved.

  2. Now, you're ready to register!
    • Visit Vote.USA.gov to see if you can register online.
    • If you can't register online, visit this link to download the "National Mail Voter Registration Form," which can be filled out by hand and mailed in to the location listed for your state in the PDF file.
  3. Once you've registered to vote, visit this page to find the date of your next local or federal election!

How do I know who to vote for?!?!?

Ultimately whom you vote for is up to you. It's a personal choice, and you shouldn't feel pressured by your friends, family, or community. That said, it's very important to remain informed! Use the links below to research candidates in your area so you can make an informed decision.

  • Take this quiz to find your candidate soulmate in the most recent or upcoming Federal election.
  • Use Vote Smart's interactive map to compare yourself to Congressional candidates.
  • Use Vote Smart to find non-partisan information about candidates in your area.
  • "On The Issues" provides an online "VoteMatch" quiz you can use to evaluate candidates at the Federal, Congressional, Gubernatorial, and even Mayoral levels.
